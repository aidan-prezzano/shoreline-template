Story

A pool conceived as a single line of water within an adobe courtyard.

The clients came to us with an unusual brief: they wanted a pool that, from the house, would read as a mirror — not as an object. The architecture already existed. The olive tree already existed. Our work was to find the one correct place for the water.

The site is a low-walled adobe courtyard, inward-facing, with a single established olive at its northern edge. The light falls evenly through most of the day; the courtyard holds warmth into the evening. A conventional pool — with a spa, a shallow end, an entry step — would have shattered the composition. So we proposed a twenty-four-meter rectangle, depth-uniform, with a single lap-swim lane and no visible equipment. Everything — pumps, heaters, filtration — sits behind the far wall, accessed from outside the courtyard entirely.

The plaster is hand-troweled in a bone tone matched to the adobe, not to the water. In the noon hours the pool reads nearly white. In the evening, it takes the sky. We specified a flush, tight-cut limestone coping ground to zero bullnose so the edge disappears. Entry is a single submerged bench the length of one wall.

"We interviewed four builders. Shoreline was the only one who walked our property before proposing anything. The finished pool feels like it was always meant to be there." Elena & Marcus R. — Clients

The most consequential decision was the smallest one. The original architectural drawings placed the pool centered between the courtyard walls. We moved it 38 inches south — off-axis from the house, but aligned with the olive. You feel the shift before you see it.

Construction took twenty-two months. We poured the shell in three sequential pours to avoid any cold joints within sight of the waterline. The equipment vault was excavated first, waterproofed with a secondary liner system, and commissioned before a single interior finish was touched — a sequence we use on every project now.
